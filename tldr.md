A Swiss corporate-ready portfolio must be concise, scannable and credibility-rich. Start with a clear hero (name/title/value proposition), then essential sections: Profile/Approach, Technical Capabilities, Selected Work (case studies), Current Focus or Vision, Engineering Principles, and Contact. Each section headline and copy should sound mature and businesslike (no youthful slang). Emphasise results and reliability, not flashy UI. Use quantifiable trust signals: project metrics, client/testimonial quotes, known technologies, standards (e.g. “GDPR-compliant”, server security), and any real client/company names or charts. Follow information hierarchy (hero → profile → skills → projects → contact); include visuals like architecture diagrams or screenshots in case studies.

On design: a light neutral palette, professional typography (serif for headings, readable sans for body), high contrast (≥4.5:1) and clear spacing. Make it responsive (mobile-first), performance-optimised (Core Web Vitals friendly
), with proper meta tags (unique title/description) and SEO-friendly filenames/alt text. Comply with EU/Swiss privacy laws (GDPR/DPA): show a privacy policy link and consent banner for cookies

.

Bad examples: long intro posts, irrelevant personal blogs, excessive projects. Avoid claims without evidence (OrbitMedia: too many unsubstantiated claims reduce credibility

). Instead use succinct bullets, real numbers (e.g. “reduced invoice errors by 60%”), and show code or architecture glimpses if available.

90-day Launch Plan: Start with user research (30 interviews), then drafting content, then build MVP site (one-page SPA or static) with analytics. Key KPIs: interview count, sign-ups/pilots, site engagement. Use A/B tests (e.g. variation in hero copy or CTA wording) to measure trust (conversion clicks, form submits).

1. Essential Sections & Their Purpose

Hero (above the fold): Company-standard large heading with your name/title (e.g. “Kenta Waibel – Systems-Oriented Software Engineer”), and a one-sentence value proposition. Purpose: immediately communicate who you are and what you do. The TL;DR of your site. Include a CTA or anchor (e.g. “View Profile”).

Profile / About: A brief narrative (2–3 short paragraphs) stating your professional identity and approach. For a Swiss corporate audience, mention systems thinking, reliability, and business focus. Example: “I build multi-user business systems emphasizing clarity and auditability.” This section builds trust by showing maturity and domain focus

.

Technical Capabilities (Skills): A structured bullet list (3–5 per category) of core competencies (e.g. “System Architecture”, “Data Engineering”, “Backend Development”). Use concise phrases (e.g. “Role-Based Access Control design”, “Relational data modeling”). The goal is quick skimmability. Avoid generic buzzwords; align skills with your niche (e.g. property mgmt systems). This matches NN/g advice to “prioritise what you want to communicate”

.

Selected Work / Case Studies: Highlight 2–4 projects demonstrating relevant skills. For each, provide Problem – Your Role – Context – Outcome. Include metrics if possible (e.g. “reduced invoice processing time by 70%”). Use subheads and bullets or table cells for these fields. If using screenshots or diagrams (see below), label them “Figure”. The purpose is to show real value delivered, not just claim skills

.

Current Direction / Vision: A short section stating your present focus or services. For example: “Currently developing audit-ready financial workflows for Swiss property management.” This signals a coherent trajectory. Position it as an evolution of your work, not a random side interest.

Engineering Principles: List your guiding principles (e.g. “Traceability, Integration over Disruption, Stability”). Each with one sentence explanation. This reassures that you follow rigorous, business-friendly design (especially for a Swiss audience that values Swiss-like thoroughness).

Trust Signals / Credentials: Integrate logos or mentions of any clients/companies you’ve worked for, technology certifications (e.g. “ISO/IEC 27001”, “GDPR-compliant”), open-source contributions, or media mentions. Don’t hide them: Orbit Media advises placing badges on high-traffic pages

. If you have testimonials (even from mentors or professors), quote one or two with name/title. Example:

    “Kenta’s architecture designs transformed our tracking system – precise and reliable.” – Anna Meier, Head of IT, Zurich Properties

Contact / Footer: Provide a clear call-to-action: “Let’s talk” or “Contact me”. Include email and LinkedIn. Possibly a short form (ensure GDPR-compliant; see Technical section). Reiterate location (e.g. Zürich) and copyright or last updated date.

5. Visual & UX Guidelines

    Typography: A modern serif for headings (e.g. Lora, as used), and an easy-reading sans-serif for body (e.g. Inter). Use 18–24px for paragraph text, 32–48px for headings on desktop.
    Color: High-contrast neutrals. E.g. dark gray/black text on white/off-white background (GOV.UK uses #0b0c0c on white

for 21:1 contrast). Accent color (like navy or teal) for headings/links to inject personality. Always maintain contrast ≥4.5:1 for text
.
Spacing: Comfortable margins and line-height (~1.5) to avoid clutter. At least 40–60px vertical spacing between sections. Align content to a central column (~600–800px wide) for readability.
Images/Graphics: Use diagrams or screenshots sparingly and only if they add value. Optimize all images (WebP preferred) and serve with width/srcset to improve Core Web Vitals

    . Include concise alt text describing each image’s purpose (for accessibility and SEO).
    Accessibility: Use semantic HTML (<nav>, <section>, headings). Ensure link text is descriptive (“Contact” rather than “Click here”). Provide a focus outline for links/buttons. The color scheme should not rely on color alone to convey meaning. Include a high-contrast mode toggle if you foresee any stylistic inversions.
    Microinteractions: Subtle hover effects on links/buttons can improve polish (e.g. underline or color shift). But avoid excessive animation that could distract or slow the site.

6. SEO & Metadata Essentials

    Title Tag: For SEO, include name and role: e.g. <title>Kenta Waibel – Software Engineer (Zurich | Property Management Systems)</title>. This signals location and expertise.
    Meta Description: A concise summary (155–160 characters) highlighting key skills/value (e.g. “Zurich-based developer building secure financial workflow software. Systems engineer skilled in Supabase, Docker, audit trails…”). Follow Google’s advice: “unique, relevant, specific to page content”

and avoid keyword stuffing.
Headings (H tags): Only one H1 (your name/title in hero). Use descending order (H2 for Profile, Capabilities, etc.). This improves crawlability.
Images: Descriptive filenames (e.g. invoice-verification-diagram.jpg). Add alt text explaining images for accessibility and SEO.
Keywords: Naturally include terms like “Swiss property management software”, “enterprise systems developer”, “ERP integration”. But primarily focus on clarity.
Performance: Fast load is an SEO factor. Minimise page weight (lazy-load images, minify CSS/JS). Use Lighthouse to check Core Web Vitals targets (LCP ≤2.5s, CLS <0.1)

    .
    Mobile-friendly: Google uses mobile-first indexing; ensure responsive design passes “Mobile-Friendly Test”.
    Sitemap & Robots: If site grows, include an XML sitemap. Block search engines from any test/demo pages via robots.txt or meta robots.

7. Technical Implementation Notes

    Stack: A static site generator or frontend framework (Next.js, Astro, etc.) can work. Given your skills, Supabase + a JS framework is fine. Host on Vercel/Azure/GitHub Pages for SSL and fast CDN delivery.
    Forms: If a contact form is included, ensure data is emailed securely (e.g. using serverless functions). Or better, use simple mailto: link to avoid data collection. Swiss law requires clear consent for data collection

.
Analytics: Use a privacy-friendly tool. The Swiss guide suggests Matomo (self-hosted) so data stays in CH
. If Google Analytics used, enable IP anonymisation and short data retention
.
Security: HTTPS mandatory. Regular backups of your code. Sanitize any input fields. Set HTTP headers (CSP, XSS protection). Possibly mention “GDPR/Swiss DPA-compliant” to reassure clients. Include a link to a simple privacy policy (must name you as data controller and describe data use)
.
Cookies/Consent: Implement a cookie banner with “Accept All/Reject All/Manage” options

    to comply with GDPR/Swiss law.
    Fonts & Assets: Use web-safe fonts or Google Fonts (preload important ones). Include retina versions of any images/illustrations (2× for high-DPI).

10. Examples of Bad Portfolios

    Mistake: Overloaded homepage with personal blog posts or animations. Avoid: irrelevant chatter. [Keep it business-focused.]
    Mistake: Listing every tech buzzword without context. Avoid: such lists; instead embed skills in narrative with examples.
    Mistake: Generic stock images and no real names/metrics. Avoid: empty visuals; use real screenshots/diagrams and real data.
    Mistake: No contact info or hard-to-find email/link. Avoid: Burying “Contact” – make it prominent.
    Mistake: Slow page, missing meta tags, no mobile support. Avoid: failing performance checks – Google penalizes poor Core Web Vitals

    .

11. File Naming & Image Specs

    Naming: kenta-waibel-profile.jpg, project-X-diagram.png, etc. Use hyphens. Include primary keyword if possible (e.g. “invoice-reconciliation-system.jpg”) for SEO.
    Resolution: For photos, at least 1200px wide, saved at ~80% JPEG quality. Diagrams as SVG or PNG (text sharp). Include 2× retina images or srcset.
    Formats: Use WebP (with JPG/PNG fallback) to reduce size. Ensure images are under 200KB if possible for fast load.
    Screenshots: Annotate with arrows or labels; keep text readable. Provide captions or alt text describing each.

12. A/B Test Ideas

    Hero Copy: Test two taglines (e.g. outcome-focused vs. skill-focused) to see which yields more clicks on “Contact”.
    Profile Photo: (If using): Test with vs without a photo. Sometimes a photo increases trust, sometimes harms (if unprofessional).
    Call-to-Action: “Contact me” vs “Let’s talk about your project” for clicks.
    Testimonials: Include one vs none, measure time on page (longer means people read it).
    Layout Variation: Single vs two-column skills list, and see scroll depth.
    Measure conversions (form submits or email clicks) and engagement (time-on-page, scroll %) via analytics.
